Vitamin D3

The sunshine hormone. Why most people are deficient, optimal blood levels, and the dose that actually moves the needle.

Vitamin D3 is not a vitamin — it's a steroid hormone precursor that your body synthesizes from cholesterol when UVB light hits your skin. It regulates over 1,000 genes, influences immune function, bone density, mood, testosterone, and cancer risk. Yet 42% of US adults are deficient (Forrest & Stuhldreher, 2011), and the number rises to 82% in Black Americans and 63% in Hispanic Americans due to melanin's UVB-blocking effect. The RDA of 600 IU is widely considered inadequate by longevity physicians. Testing your blood level and supplementing to 40-60 ng/mL is one of the simplest, highest-ROI health interventions available.

01
Why You're Probably Deficient
Indoor lifestyle, latitude, sunscreen, melanin, and aging all suppress D3 synthesis.
Modern indoor lifestyle means minimal UVB exposure — your body needs direct UVB light (not through glass) on large skin surfaces to synthesize meaningful D3. Office workers, northern latitudes (above 37°N from November-March), sunscreen use, and clothing all block synthesis. If you're not regularly outside with significant skin exposed at solar noon, you're likely deficient.
Blood test is essential — 25-hydroxyvitamin D (25(OH)D) is the definitive test. Optimal range: 40-60 ng/mL (Holick, 2007). The RDA targets 20 ng/mL, which prevents rickets but is suboptimal for immune function, mood, and longevity. Most functional medicine practitioners target 40-60.
Darker skin requires more sun — melanin is a natural sunscreen. Black Americans need 3-6x more UVB exposure to synthesize the same D3 as lighter-skinned individuals. This makes supplementation especially critical for people with darker skin tones.
Aging reduces synthesis — a 70-year-old produces ~75% less D3 from the same sun exposure as a 20-year-old due to reduced 7-dehydrocholesterol in aging skin. Supplementation becomes increasingly important with age.
💊
02
The Supplementation Protocol
5,000 IU D3 + K2. Take with fat. Test quarterly. Adjust to 40-60 ng/mL.
Standard starting dose: 5,000 IU D3 daily — this is the dose most functional medicine practitioners recommend for adults with baseline levels below 40 ng/mL. Some individuals need more (up to 10,000 IU) based on body weight, absorption, and baseline levels. Always titrate based on blood tests.
Always pair with Vitamin K2 (MK-7) — D3 increases calcium absorption from your gut. Without K2, that calcium can deposit in arteries instead of bones. K2 (specifically MK-7 form, 100-200mcg/day) directs calcium to bones and teeth where it belongs. This is non-negotiable.
Take with fat for absorption — D3 is fat-soluble. Taking it with a meal containing fat increases absorption by 50%+. Take it with breakfast or your fattiest meal of the day.
Test every 3-6 months until stable — get a 25(OH)D blood test after 3 months of supplementation to confirm you're in the 40-60 ng/mL range. Once stable, test annually. Adjust dose based on results — don't just guess.

🔗 Stacking — Synergies

💪
+ K2 (MK-7)
Non-negotiable pairing. 100-200mcg MK-7 daily directs calcium to bones, not arteries.
🦴
+ Magnesium
Mg is required for D3 metabolism. Many people are deficient in both. Supplement together.
🧬
+ Omega-3
D3 + omega-3s have synergistic anti-inflammatory effects. Take together with a fatty meal.
🏋️
+ Training
Adequate D3 supports testosterone, muscle function, and bone density for training.
☀️
+ Morning sun
10 min morning sunlight for circadian benefit + natural D3 synthesis when UVB is available.

✅ Daily Checklist

Took D3 supplement with a fat-containing meal
Took K2 (MK-7) alongside D3
Got morning sunlight (10+ min, circadian + D3 synthesis)
Scheduled or completed 25(OH)D blood test
Current blood level in 40-60 ng/mL range (or working toward it)
Took magnesium (co-factor for D3 metabolism)
Ate omega-3 rich food or supplemented EPA/DHA
Checked supplement for D3 form (cholecalciferol, not D2)
Noted mood and energy levels (D3 affects serotonin)
Sunscreen applied only after initial UVB exposure for D3 synthesis
