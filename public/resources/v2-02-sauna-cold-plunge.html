Sauna & Cold Plunge

Contrast therapy, heat shock proteins, dopamine optimization, and the cardiovascular benefits of deliberate temperature exposure.

Deliberate heat and cold exposure are two of the most powerful — and free — longevity tools available. The Finnish Kuopio KIHD Study (20+ year follow-up) showed that men using sauna 4–7x/week had a 40% reduction in cardiovascular mortality and a 24–63% lower risk of sudden cardiac death compared to once-per-week users. On the cold side, Šrámek et al. (2000) demonstrated that immersion in 14°C water increases dopamine by 250% and norepinephrine by 530% — with effects lasting 2–3 hours post-exposure. These aren't marginal biohacks. They're interventions with effect sizes rivaling pharmaceutical drugs, available to anyone with access to a sauna or cold water.

🔥
Heat
Sauna Science
Heat shock proteins, cardiovascular mimicry, immune activation, longevity signaling.
Heat shock proteins (HSPs) — sauna activates HSP70 and HSP90, which refold damaged proteins and prevent aggregation. This is the same protective pathway activated during fever. HSPs are associated with reduced neurodegeneration and improved cellular stress resilience.
Cardiovascular mimicry — sauna raises heart rate to 100–150 bpm (equivalent to moderate exercise), increases cardiac output, and improves endothelial function. A 2025 Cureus review described sauna as offering "cardiovascular benefits similar to exercise without musculoskeletal strain."
Growth hormone — sauna sessions can increase GH by 200–300% acutely, with some protocols (repeated sessions at 80°C+) showing up to 16x increases in GH. This is transient but supports recovery and body composition when combined with training.
Inflammation reduction — regular sauna users show measurably lower CRP (C-reactive protein). The Kuopio study found a dose-response relationship: more frequent and longer sessions correlated with greater inflammatory marker reduction.
Mortality data — the KIHD Study (2,315 men, 20.7yr median follow-up, JAMA 2015): 4–7x/week sauna = 40% lower CV mortality, 24% lower all-cause mortality. 15–20 min sessions showed the strongest association. This was replicated in a 2018 BMC Medicine study including both men and women.
🧊
Cold
Cold Plunge Science
Dopamine surge, norepinephrine, brown fat activation, stress inoculation.
Dopamine: 250% increase — Šrámek et al. (2000) showed immersion at 14°C (57°F) for 1 hour increased plasma dopamine by 250%. Unlike caffeine or stimulants, this is a slow, sustained elevation lasting 2–3 hours — no crash. This explains the mood and motivation lift cold plungers report.
Norepinephrine: 530% increase — the same study showed a 530% increase in norepinephrine, a neurotransmitter critical for attention, focus, and mood. This is the mechanism behind cold exposure's antidepressant-like effects and the sustained alertness reported post-plunge.
Brown fat activation — cold exposure activates brown adipose tissue (BAT), which burns calories to generate heat. Regular cold exposure increases BAT volume and activity, improving metabolic health and insulin sensitivity (Orava, 2011).
Cold shock proteins (RBM3) — cold exposure induces RBM3, a cold shock protein that promotes synaptogenesis (new neural connections). Animal studies show RBM3 protects against neurodegeneration. This is an active area of human research.
Stress inoculation — deliberate cold exposure trains your sympathetic nervous system to manage acute stress more efficiently. Over time, this translates to lower baseline cortisol and improved stress resilience across all domains — not just physical.
Protocols

🎯 Recommended Protocols

🔥 Sauna Protocol

Temperature: 170–212°F (77–100°C) traditional dry sauna. This is the modality studied in the Finnish longevity research. Infrared saunas operate at lower temps (120–150°F) and have less clinical data behind them.
Duration: 15–20 min per session (the Kuopio sweet spot for mortality benefit).
Frequency: 3–7x/week. More frequent = stronger associations with longevity outcomes.
Timing: Fit it into your routine — there's no single "best" time. Early AM sauna pairs well with morning movement and sets a strong tone for the day. Pre-workout sauna warms up muscles and joints. Post-workout sauna enhances recovery via HSPs. Rest-day sauna supports active recovery. Find the slot that makes it consistent.
Hydration: Drink 16–32oz water before and after. You lose 0.5–1L of sweat per session.

🧊 Cold Plunge Protocol

Temperature: 38–60°F (3–15°C). Colder = shorter duration needed. 50°F is a strong starting point.
Duration: 1–5 min for most people. Total of 11 min/week distributed across sessions (Huberman recommendation).
Frequency: 2–4x/week. End on cold (not hot) for maximum dopamine and norepinephrine benefit.
Breathing: Exhale on entry, slow controlled breathing throughout. Avoid hyperventilating. Nasal breathing if possible.
Post-cold: Do NOT warm up with hot shower. Let your body shiver and rewarm naturally — this is where the metabolic benefit happens.

🔄 The Contrast Protocol (Hot → Cold → Repeat)

Alternating between sauna and cold plunge amplifies the benefits of both. The vascular cycling (dilation → constriction → dilation) acts as a pump for your circulatory system.

15–20 min at 170–200°F

1–3 min at 38–55°F
Full immersion. Controlled exhale on entry. Slow breathing. The shock fades after 30 seconds.

⚠️ Safety & Contraindications

❤️Cardiovascular conditions: consult your physician before sauna or cold plunge if you have heart disease, uncontrolled hypertension, or arrhythmias. Both create acute cardiovascular stress.
🤰Pregnancy: sauna raises core body temperature, which is contraindicated in pregnancy. Cold plunge also poses risks. Avoid both during pregnancy.
🍺Never combine with alcohol: alcohol impairs thermoregulation, judgment, and cardiovascular reflexes. Sauna + alcohol is a documented cause of fatal cardiac events in Finland.
💊Blood pressure medications: both heat and cold cause acute BP changes. Beta blockers and vasodilators alter your body's thermal response. Get physician clearance.
🧊Cold water shock: the first 30 seconds of cold immersion triggers involuntary gasping. Enter slowly, exhale on contact, control breathing. Never plunge alone if inexperienced.
⏱️Don't overdo cold after training: cold immersion within 4 hrs of strength training may blunt hypertrophy adaptations. Use cold on off-days or 4+ hrs post-training for best of both worlds.

🔗 Stacking — Synergies

😴
+ Sleep
If you sauna later in the day, finish 90+ min before bed so core temp can drop. Many find AM sauna improves sleep quality that same night.
🏋️
+ Training
Pre-workout: warms muscles and joints. Post-workout: enhances recovery via HSPs. Rest days: supports active recovery. Cold on off-days or 4+ hrs post-lifting.
🫁
+ Breathwork
Wim Hof breathing before cold plunge increases CO2 tolerance and reduces cold shock response.
🧘
+ Meditation
The cold plunge is forced presence. Post-plunge meditation capitalizes on elevated focus and calm.
🛡️
+ Glutathione
Heat stress increases oxidative load. Adequate GSH protects mitochondria during sauna exposure.
